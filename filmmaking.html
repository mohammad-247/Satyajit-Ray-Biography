Filmmaking

Satyajit Ray’s filmmaking journey truly began with his masterpiece — “Pather Panchali” (Song of the Little Road, 1955) — his directorial debut and the first film of what became known as the Apu Trilogy.

The Apu Trilogy

Pather Panchali (1955)

Based on Bibhutibhushan Bandopadhyay’s novel, Pather Panchali tells the story of Apu, a young boy growing up in rural Bengal, and his family’s struggles with poverty, dreams, and survival. Ray wanted to make a film that captured the truth of Indian village life — simple yet poetic.

However, making Pather Panchali was anything but simple. Ray faced enormous challenges:

  • He had no previous filmmaking experience.
  • Funding was nearly impossible to find — the film was financed partly by the Government of West Bengal after years of delay.
  • Most of the cast were non-professional actors.
  • Shooting took nearly three years (1952–1955) to complete.
Despite the hurdles, the result was extraordinary. The film’s realism, emotional depth, and music (composed by Pandit Ravi Shankar) touched audiences worldwide. When it premiered at the Cannes Film Festival in 1956, it won the Best Human Document Award and received a standing ovation.

Aparajito (1956)

The second film of the trilogy continues Apu’s story as he moves from childhood to adolescence. It explores his relationship with his mother, Sarbojaya, and his growing independence as he pursues education in the city.

Aparajito won the Golden Lion at the Venice Film Festival and further established Ray as a world-class director. Its emotional honesty and attention to small details — like the sound of a train or a mother’s quiet worry — became Ray’s trademarks.

Aparajito (1959)

The final part of the trilogy shows Apu as a grown man navigating love, loss, and fatherhood. Starring Soumitra Chatterjee and Sharmila Tagore (in her debut), it’s a deeply moving story about resilience and hope.

The film cemented Ray’s reputation as a master storyteller — simple, human, and profoundly emotional.


The Apu Trilogy collectively won over 30 international awards, screened at major festivals (Cannes, Venice, Berlin), and is often ranked among the greatest achievements in film history. It introduced Indian cinema to the global art scene, showing the world that films from India could be both deeply local and universally human.

This trilogy established Satyajit Ray as one of the greatest filmmakers of the 20th century, marking the birth of Indian parallel (realist) cinema.

After the global success of The Apu Trilogy, Satyajit Ray continued to explore new subjects, styles, and storytelling forms. He never repeated himself — each film had its own identity, tone, and message.

While Pather Panchali and its sequels focused on rural life and human struggle, Ray’s later films explored urban society, social change, gender roles, moral conflict, and the clash between tradition and modernity.

Jalsaghar (1958)

Ray’s first post-Apu film tells the story of Biswambhar Roy, an aging aristocrat obsessed with classical music even as his wealth and world crumble. The film is both a visual and emotional masterpiece, showing the decay of feudalism and the loneliness of a man clinging to a glorious past. The grand music sequences, composed by Ustad Vilayat Khan, remain among the most celebrated in Indian cinema.

Devi (1960)

In Devi, Ray questioned blind faith and superstition. The story follows a young woman, Doyamoyee, who is believed to be an incarnation of the goddess Kali. Her family’s devotion spirals into tragedy. This film stirred controversy in conservative circles but was praised internationally for its powerful storytelling and haunting imagery.

Mahanagar (1963)

A deeply human film about a middle-class housewife, Arati, who starts working to support her family — something uncommon in 1960s India. The film sensitively explores women’s independence, economic struggles, and social expectations, long before such topics became mainstream.

Charulata (1964)

Considered by many critics as Ray’s finest film, Charulata is based on a novella by Rabindranath Tagore. It depicts the emotional loneliness of a married woman who develops feelings for her husband’s cousin. Ray called it “the film closest to my heart.” Its graceful camera work, rich symbolism, and emotional subtlety make it a landmark in world cinema.

Nayak (1966)

A psychological drama about a film star reflecting on his life during a train journey. Starring Uttam Kumar, Bengal’s matinee idol, the film explores fame, regret, and identity. It’s both a self-reflective commentary on celebrity culture and an introspective look at the human mind.

Goopy Gyne Bagha Byne (1969)

A total change in tone — this was Ray’s first fantasy musical, based on a story written by his grandfather. It follows two village musicians who are granted magical powers by the King of Ghosts. Filled with humor, music, and imagination, it became one of India’s most beloved children’s films. Ray later made two sequels: Hirak Rajar Deshe (1980) and Goopy Bagha Phire Elo (1992).

The Calcutta Trilogy

In the 1970s, Ray turned his focus to the social unrest and moral corruption in modern urban India.

This trilogy includes:

  1. Pratidwandi (1970)– about a young graduate struggling to find a job in a chaotic city.
  2. Seemabaddha (1971)– showing the moral compromises of corporate ambition.
  3. Jana Aranya (1975)– a chilling look at how economic pressure drives ordinary men to unethical choices.
Together, these films capture the socio-political turbulence of 1970s Kolkata.

Shatranj Ke Khilari (1977)

Ray’s only Hindi feature film, starring Sanjeev Kumar, Saeed Jaffrey, and Amjad Khan, set in 1856 Lucknow. It depicts two noblemen obsessed with chess, ignoring the British annexation of their kingdom. The film blends history, irony, and political commentary — showing Ray’s mastery beyond Bengali cinema.

Later Films

In the 1980s and early 1990s, Ray’s health declined, but he continued working. Films like:

  • Ghare-Baire (1984)
  • Ganashatru (1989)
  • Shakha Proshakha (1990)
  • Agantuk (1991) — (his final film) — explored philosophy, idealism, and human values.
Even in his final years, Ray’s cinema retained its clarity, empathy, and moral vision.
