Early Career

After completing his education at Santiniketan, Satyajit Ray returned to Calcutta and started his professional life as a commercial artist. In 1943, he joined the British advertising agency D.J. Keymer (which later became Ogilvy & Mather) as a junior visualizer.

At first, Ray wasn’t fond of advertising, calling it “a necessary compromise,” but the job taught him valuable skills in design, layout, and visual communication — all of which he later applied to his film posters, credit titles, and compositions.

While working in advertising, he also did freelance design work for the newly founded publishing house Signet Press, where he designed some of the most iconic book covers in Bengali literature. Among them was Bibhutibhushan Bandopadhyay’s novel “Pather Panchali”, whose story about a poor rural family deeply moved him — and would later inspire his first film.

In 1947, Ray co-founded the Calcutta Film Society along with Chidananda Das Gupta and other film enthusiasts. This was a turning point — the society screened world cinema classics that were otherwise unavailable in India, allowing Ray to study the works of filmmakers like Jean Renoir, Charlie Chaplin, Vittorio De Sica, and Sergei Eisenstein.

In 1949, French filmmaker Jean Renoir came to India to shoot The River. Ray met him and assisted him in location scouting. Renoir encouraged him to follow his dream of becoming a filmmaker and advised him to stay true to Indian themes rather than imitating Western styles.

A year later, during a visit to London on a work trip for his ad agency, Ray watched Vittorio De Sica’s Italian neorealist film “Bicycle Thieves” (1948) — a film about ordinary people struggling in post-war poverty. Ray was deeply moved. He later said, “I came out of the theater determined to become a filmmaker.”

That moment solidified his vision: to make films about real people and real India, not fantasy or melodrama — and that dream became Pather Panchali.
