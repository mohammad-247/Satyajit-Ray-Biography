Literary Contribution

While Satyajit Ray is world-renowned as a filmmaker, in Bengal, he is equally celebrated as one of the most popular writers of modern Bengali literature, especially for children and young adults. His writing is known for its imagination, humor, suspense, and simplicity — qualities that made his stories timeless and loved by all ages.

Revival of Sandesh Magazine

One of Satyajit Ray’s lesser-known yet deeply meaningful achievements was his revival of the children’s magazine Sandesh, a publication that had been an integral part of his family’s literary heritage.

Sandesh was originally founded in 1913 by his grandfather, Upendrakishore Ray Chowdhury, a pioneering writer, illustrator, and printer. The magazine quickly became one of Bengal’s most loved children’s journals, known for its imaginative stories, beautiful illustrations, and focus on science, art, and moral values.

After Upendrakishore’s death, the magazine was briefly managed by Ray’s father, Sukumar Ray, who infused it with his trademark humor, wit, and nonsense rhymes. However, following Sukumar’s untimely death in 1923, Sandesh gradually stopped publishing.

Decades later, in 1961, to mark the centenary of Rabindranath Tagore’s birth, Satyajit Ray decided to revive Sandesh with the help of his close friends and family. Ray took charge as editor, illustrator, and designer, giving the magazine a fresh yet nostalgic identity.

He introduced modern printing techniques, redesigned the logo and layout, and wrote and illustrated many stories himself — including the first Feluda and Professor Shonku tales. The magazine continued to uphold the same spirit of curiosity, creativity, and moral depth that his grandfather envisioned.

Under Ray’s guidance, Sandesh became more than just a children’s magazine — it became a cultural bridge that connected generations of Bengali readers with science, imagination, and storytelling.

Even today, Sandesh continues to publish, carrying forward the Ray family legacy of enriching young minds through literature, art, and science.

Feluda

Among Satyajit Ray’s most popular literary creations stands Pradosh Chandra Mitter, better known as Feluda — the sharp, charming private detective from Kolkata. He first appeared in the short story Feludar Goyendagiri (1965), published in Sandesh, the children’s magazine founded by Ray’s grandfather.

Feluda’s character was inspired by Ray’s love for logic, deduction, and observation. Like Sherlock Holmes, Feluda uses intelligence, reasoning, and minute attention to detail to solve mysteries — but unlike Holmes, he is deeply rooted in Indian culture, manners, and values.

Feluda is usually accompanied by his teenage cousin Tapesh Ranjan Mitter (Topshe), who narrates the stories, and their comic friend, the writer Lalmohan Ganguly (Jatayu), whose humorous presence balances Feluda’s serious nature.

The stories are known for their Bengali flavor, travel descriptions, witty dialogue, and moral depth. Ray combined thrilling mysteries with rich cultural settings — from the deserts of Rajasthan to the ghats of Varanasi and the streets of Kathmandu.

Two of his most famous stories, Sonar Kella and Joi Baba Felunath, were made into films directed by Ray himself. Both became all-time classics, making Feluda a household name in Bengal and beyond.

Even decades later, Feluda remains one of India’s most beloved fictional detectives — smart, ethical, modern, and unmistakably Indian.

Professor Sonku

Another fascinating creation of Satyajit Ray is Professor Trilokeshwar Shonku, a brilliant scientist, inventor, and adventurer whose stories belong to the realm of science fiction and fantasy.

Shonku, who lives in Giridih with his loyal servant Prahlad, his pet cat Newton, and his robot Bidushekhar, creates astonishing inventions — from Miracurall (a universal cure) to Annihilin (a weapon of destruction). His adventures often take him across continents — from the Amazon rainforest to Tibet, Mars, and even alien planets.

Ray wrote the Shonku stories in the form of diary entries, giving them a realistic, scientific tone while keeping the language simple and engaging. His attention to scientific detail, combined with moral questions about knowledge, power, and ethics, made these stories ahead of their time.

Through Shonku, Ray explored the wonders and dangers of science — showing both curiosity and caution. The stories reflected his fascination with space exploration, technology, and the unknown, blending imagination with credibility.

The Professor Shonku series helped shape Bengali science fiction and continues to inspire new generations of readers, scientists, and writers. His adventures are a testament to Ray’s ability to mix intellect, imagination, and storytelling genius.

Ray’s writing style was simple yet elegant, with a focus on clarity and imagination. His stories carried a moral tone without preaching — they entertained, educated, and provoked thought.
